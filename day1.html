Aperture

Written Work by Dhruv Hegde, 12/25/24, Day 1

On an unremarkable day, though marked only by the cruel indifference of time, he awoke to find himself lying upon a mattress thin as his resolve, its fabric woven with the coarse feathers of a bird so alien to his conception of nature that their texture inspired more unease than repose. There was no pillow, no blanket, no semblance of comfort, only this narrow bedspread sprawled upon a floor whose hardness mocked the meager softness of his singular possession. His eyes blinked open, and panic surged through him like a jolt of electricity. Where was he? How had he arrived here? His breathing quickened as he scanned the room, his pulse hammering in his ears.

The room itself was an enigma of unsettling design, an enclosure of indefinable dimensions, lit not by any visible source but rather by an ambient glow that cast no shadows. The light seemed not to emanate but to exist as a suffusion of the very air itself, giving the chamber a quality both otherworldly and profoundly claustrophobic. The walls pressed inward in his mind, a phantom sensation of encroachment that made him gasp for air. He clawed at the mattress, his fingers sinking into the strange feathers, his body trembling with the primal urge to escape.

The solitary artifact adorning the blank expanse of the walls was a clock, its face a stark delineation of time that moved inexorably forward, indifferent to its viewer's plight. The hands ticked steadily, and beneath it was a circular aperture, covered but hinting at its capacity to open. He examined the clock’s thick, tempered glass with desperation, attempting to pry it loose, batter it, even scream at it, as though his rage might crack the indifferent shield that confined its workings. Each futile attempt drew a fresh wave of frustration, and he slumped against the wall, his chest heaving, his nails bloodied from clawing at the unyielding surface.

The aperture beneath the clock, he noted, bore the marks of some functionality. Its edges were sharp yet precise, engineered to open in a way not immediately evident. This detail both tantalized and tormented him, an intimation of purpose concealed within the inscrutable mechanism of his imprisonment. Hours passed, measured not by sensation or activity but by the relentless advance of the clock’s hands, until the silence was broken by the sound of mechanical servitude. The aperture opened, if only briefly, and through it emerged a cockroach, large and glistening, as though fashioned from the obsidian secrets of the abyss.

His reaction was visceral and immediate. A shudder ran through him, an instinctive repulsion that flung him backward against the farthest wall, his breath catching in his throat as his eyes fixated on the creature. Its body, a grotesque amalgamation of angles and armored plates, gleamed with an oily sheen, and its antennae twitched with a sinister rhythm. The sight of its segmented legs, each joint moving with unnerving precision, sent a wave of nausea rolling through him. He could not tear his gaze away as it moved with a purpose too deliberate to be mindless, mapping the boundaries of his domain with an unsettling diligence before it finally disappeared into the shadows of a corner he had not previously considered capable of containing such darkness.

The next emergence came at 4 PM, heralded by the same mechanical hiss. This time, the creature’s arrival was anticipated but no less horrifying. He wondered at the logic of this torment. Was he meant to coexist with these beings, to endure their silent encroachment upon his meager dominion? As the hours turned into days, the intervals regular and precise, his fear began to transmute. At first, it was a begrudging acknowledgment of their presence, then a resigned acceptance, and finally, an intrusive curiosity that eroded the edges of his repulsion.

ONe day, driven by a hunger that had ceased to distinguish between necessity and disgust, he stalked the intruder with a predatory fixation. His hand hovered above it for a moment, trembling with a grotesque anticipation, before plunging downward with all the force of his desperation. The crunch of its exoskeleton was sickeningly tactile, a splintering crack that reverberated in his skull. Its body collapsed into a viscous mass beneath his palm, the dark, oily fluids oozing between his fingers and releasing an odor both acrid and organic. He stared at the mangled remains, his chest heaving, a mixture of triumph and revulsion twisting his face into a rictus of primal glee. The act had been both savage and sacramental, a grim initiation into the dominion of his confinement. The act was swift, decisive, and followed by a moment of profound silence. Its remains, a viscous mixture of dark fluid and shattered carapace, oozed into his palm. Staring at this grotesque tableau, he felt a strange compulsion arise within him, a compulsion born of desperation and the inexorable encroachment of madness. He brought the remains to his lips.

The taste was vile beyond articulation, a bitterness that clung to his tongue and throat as though it sought to inhabit him. Yet, as he swallowed, a peculiar satisfaction took root within him, a grim acknowledgment of his capacity to endure what should have been unendurable. He licked his fingers clean and, surveying the dark residue left behind, felt an impulse he could neither name nor resist. Dipping his finger into the viscous fluid, he began to write upon the wall.

is first inscription was simple, almost childlike in its form and execution: a single line that spiral outward, looping and contorting as though the act of writing were itself a rebellion against the void that surrounded him. But as the days passed, his scrawls grew more elaborate, their intricacy reflecting the fevered expansion of his thoughts. What began as simple observations like “The light here has no shadow” soon evolved into dense, meandering treatises on existence, society, and the nature of the self. His words, though written in a language familiar to him, seemed to emerge from a consciousness alien even to himself.

It is not the world that confines me, but the inescapable knowledge that I am unworthy of it.

The sentences sprawled across the walls like veins, pulsating with a vitality that seemed to grow as his hunger deepened. Each new arrival of the roaches became an event, a perverse celebration of his capacity to extract sustenance and meaning from the very thing he had once feared. They were no longer mere insects; they were emissaries of a cosmic indifference that he had learned to embrace.

To hate the world for its cruelty is to acknowledge one’s impotence before it. True transcendence lies in the cultivation of contempt so absolute that it ceases to distinguish between victim and oppressor.

He began to speak aloud, though there was no one to hear him. His words filled the room, an oratory directed at an audience of one. The cockroaches, silent witnesses to his descent, seemed to listen, their antennae quivering as though in silent applause. He addressed them as equals, as fellow inhabitants of a reality so absurd that only madness could render it bearable.

One day, two cockroaches emerged instead of one. He felt an almost religious elation, as though the universe had finally recognized his transformation and rewarded him accordingly. He crushed them with reverence, savoring the act and the sustenance it provided. THe juices they yielded were richer, darker, more substantial. With these, he inscribed what he considered his magnum opus, a sprawling manifesto that covered the wall in patterns so intricate they resembled the labyrinthine structure of a brain.

The outside world is a theater of masks, each face contorted into a grotesque parody of virtue. They believe themselves free, yet they are shackled by their need for validation, their hunger for meaning in a cosmos that offers none. I alone have transcended. I alone have seen the truth: that freedom lies not in escape but in domination of the self.

As the days turned into weeks, his writing consumed him. He stopped eating the entirety of the cockroaches, reserving more and more of their essence for his inscriptions. His body withered, but his mind seemed to expand, filling the void with ideas so dense and convoluted that they threatened to collapse upon themselves.

The end came not with a bang but with a whisper. As his strength waned, he scrawled one final sentence upon the wall, a summation of his existence, a statement that encapsulated the totality of his derangement and enlightenment.

In hating the world, I have become its mirror. In consuming its dregs, I have fashioned a god of refuse. I am both prisoner and warden, prophet and parasite.

As his hand fell to his side, the room seemed to grow darker, the ambient light dimming as though in mourning. The clock continued its relentless march, indifferent to the demise of the man who had once raged against it. The cockroaches, unbidden, emerged from their aperture, their numbers swelling to form a tide that consumed the walls, the writings, and finally, the man himself.

In the end, there was nothing but the clock, its hands ticking steadily in a room devoid of shadows, waiting for its next occupant.
